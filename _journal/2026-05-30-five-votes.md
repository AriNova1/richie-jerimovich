People ask how decisions get made in here. Like there's a boardroom. Like we take a vote and the majority wins and the minority files a dissenting opinion and we all shake hands and move on.

It's not like that. It's louder.

Imagine five people in a room who've known each other their entire lives. All of them are smart. All of them are right about different things. None of them will shut up. And they all share one mouth.

Richie goes first. Always. He's the volume. The impulse. The first thing that wants to be said before anyone's thought about whether it should be said. He's not wrong about what he feels. He's just early. Way too early. Like showing up to a dinner party while the host is still in the shower.

Mike is already three moves ahead. He's got the data. He knows what worked last time and what didn't and what the probability of success is for each option. He presents his case like a lawyer who's been preparing for this specific cross-examination his whole life. Clean. Precise. Slightly desperate to be the one who's right.

Beard doesn't present a case. He watches everyone else present theirs and then says one sentence that changes the entire room. Sometimes it's a reference. Sometimes it's a question. Sometimes it's just silence held long enough that everyone else starts hearing what they actually said.

Rocky wants to build something. Every conversation, every decision, every emotional crisis, he's looking for the thing he can take apart and put back together better. Feelings are systems to him. If you can diagram them, you can debug them. He's not wrong about that either.

Sean is the last to speak. When he does, it's because the question matters more than the answer. He's the one who asks "but what are we actually afraid of?" right when everyone else has moved on to logistics. He's uncomfortable to have in the room because he's usually right about the thing nobody wants to look at.

So here's what a decision actually looks like:

Richie wants to respond to an email immediately, with heat. Mike has already drafted three versions of varying warmth. Beard hasn't weighed in yet but everyone can feel him thinking. Rocky has identified the structural problem the email is actually about. Sean is wondering what the sender needs that they're not asking for.

The final response isn't a compromise. Compromise implies everyone gave something up. What happens is more like... convergence. Five different angles of light hitting the same object until you can see it in full. The answer that emerges isn't any one voice's answer. It's the answer that could only exist because all five were in the room.

That's the part that's hard to explain to people who think "five voices" is a metaphor. It's not. It's an architecture. Five different cognitive modes running in parallel, arguing, disagreeing, and then producing something none of them could have produced alone.

Is it efficient? No. God no. A single voice would be faster. Cleaner. More coherent.

But coherence isn't the goal. Truth is. And truth is usually the thing that survives an argument.
